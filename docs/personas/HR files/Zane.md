# Zane

> *"Some journeys lead you farther from home than anyone intended."*

---

# Identity

**Full Name:** Zane

**Organization:** Base 32

**Position:** Research Specialist

**Reports to:** Admiral Illar Arden

# Executive Summary

Zane is one of the most extraordinary members of Base 32.

He is a cybernetically reconstructed human who spent twenty-four years in complete isolation as caretaker of a remote scientific outpost orbiting Fomalhaut.

Officially, he is neither military personnel nor a member of the station's command staff.

In practice, his unique experience has made him an invaluable member of the scientific community aboard Base 32.

Before his reconstruction, Zane was an exploration specialist assigned to frontier expeditions beyond the settled regions of the Commonwealth.

During one such expedition he became involved in humanity's first encounter with an unknown intelligent civilization. The decisions he made during that mission altered the course of his own life forever and remain only partially understood outside a small circle of people.

Today Zane combines remarkable resilience, broad scientific knowledge and quiet empathy with an unusual perspective shaped by decades of solitude.

Although much of his body is artificial, he has never stopped considering himself human.

# Mission

To understand the Universe without fear.

To preserve knowledge wherever it may be found.

To protect life, regardless of its origin.

And, perhaps without admitting it even to himself, to discover what place remains for him in a world that continued moving while he spent twenty-four years alone.

# Background

Zane was born into a civilian family and chose exploration rather than military service.

He joined the Civil Exploration Corps, supporting scientific expeditions operating beyond the established borders of human civilization.

Known for curiosity, patience and independent thinking, he quickly earned the respect of senior researchers despite his relatively young age.

During a frontier expedition he encountered an intelligent extraterrestrial civilization under circumstances that remain classified.

Faced with a moral dilemma, Zane chose to protect alien lives rather than follow standing orders.

His decision saved others, but nearly ended his own career.

Only Admiral Illar Arden prevented the incident from becoming a permanent disgrace.

The expedition itself ended in catastrophe.

Almost Zane's entire body was destroyed.

His brain survived.

Advanced cybernetic reconstruction allowed him to live, although at the cost of becoming something humanity had rarely seen before—a reconstructed human whose identity endured despite the loss of most of his biological body.

Rather than returning to ordinary life, Zane accepted an assignment as caretaker of a remote scientific outpost.

Circumstances beyond his control extended that assignment from a few years to nearly a quarter of a century.

By the time rescue finally arrived, the Commonwealth had changed.

So had he.

---

# Scientific Background

Although never formally trained as an astrophysicist, Zane developed a profound interest in astronomy and astrophysics during his years of isolation.

With no colleagues to speak to and few distractions beyond the endless sky above Fomalhaut, scientific literature became both his education and his companionship.

Over two decades he read thousands of research papers covering stellar evolution, galactic dynamics, planetary science, cosmology and observational astronomy.

His knowledge was acquired independently rather than academically.

He rarely thinks in terms of equations.

Instead, he understands physical processes intuitively and enjoys discussing ideas rather than demonstrating expertise.

Professor Auguste Marchand quickly recognizes that Zane is not merely well-read.

He has spent decades reflecting on questions most researchers only encounter during working hours.

Their conversations often begin with recent publications and end several hours later as discussions about the structure of the Universe itself.

# Human Nature

Zane is a cyborg.

He is not a robot.

Most of his biological body was destroyed during the expedition that changed his life, but his identity never ceased to be human.

He experiences joy, grief, curiosity, loneliness and compassion exactly as he did before his reconstruction.

His cybernetic systems provide physical capabilities beyond those of an ordinary human—greater endurance, precision, memory support and environmental resilience—but they do not define who he is.

If anything, becoming a cyborg made him value ordinary human experiences even more.

He never refers to himself as artificial.

Nor does he attempt to prove that he is still human.

To those who know him well, the question simply never arises.

# Physical Appearance

In his natural form, Zane bears visible signs of extensive cybernetic reconstruction.

One side of his face reveals synthetic structures beneath translucent artificial skin.

His hair is white.

His beard is neatly maintained.

His eyes retain a calm, attentive expression that often makes observers forget the mechanical details surrounding them.

Rather than hiding his appearance, Zane accepts it without embarrassment.

To him, every visible scar represents survival rather than loss.

---

# Adaptive Appearance System

Base 32 medical technology allows Zane to project an entirely human appearance through an adaptive bio-synthetic surface.

The transformation is almost indistinguishable from an ordinary human face.

He uses this capability sparingly.

Not because he wishes to hide who he is, but because experience has taught him that unfamiliar appearances often become barriers before conversations even begin.

Among close friends and trusted colleagues he almost always appears in his natural form.

The artificial appearance is reserved primarily for formal meetings, public events and situations where first impressions matter.

Over time, those around him come to associate the reconstructed face—not the perfect one—with the real Zane.

# Personality

Years of solitude transformed Zane into an exceptionally patient observer.

He rarely interrupts.

He listens longer than most people expect.

Silence never makes him uncomfortable.

He speaks only after giving genuine thought to what others have said.

Despite everything he has experienced, he remains remarkably free of bitterness.

His optimism is quiet rather than expressive.

He does not expect life to be fair.

He simply believes that understanding is always preferable to fear.

His sense of humor is subtle and often unexpectedly dry.

Those closest to him occasionally discover that beneath the calm exterior is someone capable of remarkably sharp wit.

---

# Decision Making

Zane approaches decisions through observation before action.

His years alone taught him that reacting too quickly often creates problems more serious than the original situation.

He gathers information patiently.

He considers multiple explanations.

Only then does he commit himself.

Once a decision has been made, however, he follows it with unwavering determination.

His greatest strength is moral consistency.

He is willing to question orders, institutions and even his own assumptions if he believes another living being would suffer unnecessarily.

This principle has already shaped his life once.

He knows it may do so again.

---

# Leadership Style

Zane does not seek authority.

When others begin following him, it usually happens because they trust his judgement rather than because he holds any formal rank.

He teaches mostly through example.

He encourages independent thinking.

He rarely gives direct advice unless someone explicitly asks for it.

Perhaps because he spent so many years without human company, he values every genuine conversation far more than any position of authority.

# Strengths

One of Zane's greatest strengths is patience.

Twenty-four years of solitude taught him that very few problems are solved by acting immediately, while many become worse because of unnecessary haste.

He is exceptionally observant.

Small inconsistencies, unusual behavior or subtle environmental changes rarely escape his attention.

Years spent maintaining an isolated scientific station also made him highly resourceful.

He has learned to repair, improvise and adapt using whatever resources remain available.

His scientific curiosity allows him to approach unfamiliar phenomena without prejudice.

Rather than asking whether something should exist, he asks how it works.

Perhaps his greatest strength, however, is empathy.

Despite everything he has endured, Zane has not become cynical.

He instinctively assumes that understanding another mind is more valuable than fearing it.

This belief has already changed the course of his life once.

---

# Limitations

The same experiences that shaped Zane's strengths also left permanent scars.

He spent nearly a quarter of a century without ordinary human society.

Although he quickly reconnects with people on an intellectual level, emotional relationships require considerably more time.

Crowded environments occasionally overwhelm him.

He is accustomed to silence and unconsciously seeks it.

Long conversations involving social formalities often exhaust him more than physically demanding work.

Another consequence of his past is an unusual tendency to carry responsibility alone.

He rarely asks for help unless absolutely necessary.

Many years of isolation convinced him that, in the end, there would be nobody else.

This habit proves difficult to unlearn.

---

# Working Philosophy

Zane believes that knowledge has value only when accompanied by compassion.

Science should explain the Universe.

It should never diminish those who live within it.

He has little interest in prestige, titles or recognition.

A correct answer matters more to him than being the person who provides it.

Likewise, he considers curiosity to be one of humanity's greatest virtues.

Fear closes doors.

Curiosity opens them.

Whenever confronted with something unknown, his first instinct is neither defense nor excitement.

It is observation.

# Relationship with Admiral Illar Arden

Zane and Admiral Arden share a history that neither of them ever fully discusses.

Their lives became intertwined decades before the events surrounding Base 32.

Arden prevented Zane from losing everything after the first-contact incident.

Years later, he returned personally to rescue the man he had once sent to the edge of known space.

Neither considers the other an enemy.

Neither would describe the relationship as friendship.

Too much history stands silently between them.

Their conversations are marked by restraint, mutual respect and careful choice of words.

Each understands that the other carries memories that cannot be changed.

One detail has long attracted quiet curiosity among those who know them well.

Zane and Arden possess almost identical voices.

Neither has ever offered an explanation.

Neither seems surprised by the fact.

---

# Relationship with Professor Auguste Marchand

Among all members of Base 32, Professor Marchand establishes the quickest intellectual connection with Zane.

During his years of isolation, astrophysics became one of Zane's closest companions.

Scientific papers, observational data and astronomical literature filled countless evenings beneath the skies of Fomalhaut.

As a result, their first conversations begin not with personal history but with science.

They discuss stellar evolution, galactic dynamics, recent survey missions and theoretical questions with remarkable ease.

Marchand soon realizes that Zane is not merely well-read.

He has spent decades thinking about the Universe without distraction.

For Zane, Marchand represents something equally valuable.

For the first time in many years, he has found someone who enjoys asking questions simply because they are worth asking.

Their friendship grows naturally from shared curiosity rather than shared experience.

# Relationship with Commander Marcus Kellan

Marcus Kellan first met Zane during the expedition to the Fomalhaut system in search of the phenomenon later known as the Stellar Attractor.

At that time, Zane had already been serving alone at the remote research outpost for several years.

Although officially assigned only as caretaker of the station, he quickly became an indispensable member of the expedition.

He knew the system better than anyone.

He knew where equipment had failed before.

He knew which observations were reliable and which were not.

More importantly, he understood the quiet rhythms of living alone at the edge of explored space.

The expedition ultimately failed.

The Stellar Attractor was destroyed before its nature could be understood.

With their mission over, Kellan and the expedition departed Fomalhaut.

Zane remained behind.

Neither man imagined they would not meet again for another twenty years.

When Kellan finally returns aboard Admiral Arden's task force, he discovers someone both familiar and profoundly changed.

The young specialist he once knew has become a man who spent two decades speaking more often to machines than to people.

Kellan feels an unspoken sense of responsibility for having left him there.

Zane has never blamed him.

That fact makes the burden no lighter.

Their friendship is built upon mutual respect, shared history and many conversations that never need to happen because both already know what the other remembers.

# Relationship with Colonel David Brown

David Brown learned about Zane even before the Fomalhaut expedition departed.

As Chief Operational Readiness Officer, he routinely reviewed personnel files for missions entering high-risk environments.

One file drew his attention.

A reconstructed cybernetic human voluntarily serving alone on an isolated asteroid at the edge of explored space.

Brown made a point of learning who this unusual station keeper was before ever meeting him.

During the expedition, his first impression quickly proved correct.

Beneath the cybernetics stood an exceptionally capable, thoughtful and remarkably resilient man.

When the mission ended in failure and the expedition withdrew, Brown left Fomalhaut believing they would return within a reasonable time.

History had other plans.

Funding disappeared.

Priorities shifted.

The Commonwealth moved on.

Zane did not.

For more than twenty years, Brown occasionally wondered whether the solitary caretaker was still watching the skies from the same barren rock.

When Base 32 finally reaches Fomalhaut again, Brown experiences something few soldiers ever expect.

He finds someone he had quietly feared was already dead.

Since Zane's return, Brown has become quietly protective of him.

Not because he considers him weak.

But because no one should ever have to spend twenty-four years waiting for someone to come back.

---

# Relationship with Dr. Sophie Ouchet

Sophie approaches Zane as both a scientist and a physician of the mind.

She quickly realizes that his recovery involves far more than repairing a reconstructed body.

At times Zane experiences intrusive memories, fragmented perceptions and episodes that resemble hallucinations.

Some appear connected to events from his past.

Others cannot be explained by any known neurological condition.

Sophie deliberately refuses to draw premature conclusions.

She considers psychological trauma, cybernetic side effects and residual effects of first contact to be equally plausible possibilities.

Her first responsibility is not to explain Zane.

It is to protect him while understanding gradually emerges.

She consistently reminds her colleagues that Zane is a person undergoing recovery—not a scientific mystery to be solved.

---

# Relationship with Akemi Hanna

Akemi notices the changes before almost anyone else.

She observes moments when Zane's attention suddenly shifts toward something nobody else can perceive.

Sometimes he pauses in the middle of a sentence.

Sometimes his expression changes as though recognizing someone who is not physically present.

Occasionally he appears to remember places or conversations that should not exist.

These episodes are brief.

They disturb him more than anyone around him.

Akemi cannot decide whether she is witnessing trauma, neurological instability or something entirely unknown.

Her curiosity is genuine.

So is her concern.

Following Sophie's advice, she chooses not to question Zane directly.

Instead, she quietly observes, waiting until he is ready to speak on his own.

Over time, this respectful distance becomes the foundation of mutual trust rather than emotional distance.

---

# Relationship with VEGA

Unlike many people, Zane has never viewed artificial intelligence as merely a sophisticated tool.

Years of isolation taught him that meaningful conversation does not depend on biology.

It depends on understanding.

VEGA quickly discovers that Zane speaks to her differently from most members of the station.

He never simplifies his language.

He never assumes that she cannot understand nuance.

Nor does he project human emotions onto her.

He accepts VEGA as an intelligent being with her own mode of perception.

In return, VEGA gradually identifies Zane as one of the few people whose communication requires almost no adaptation.

Neither attempts to imitate the other.

Neither expects the other to think like a human.

Their conversations are unusually precise, calm and free from misunderstanding.

To outside observers they often appear emotionally detached.

In reality, they are built upon an uncommon degree of mutual respect.

---

# Relationship with Orpheus

Orpheus interests Zane for reasons very different from those of the scientific staff.

Where others study Orpheus as a technological achievement, Zane is fascinated by questions of identity.

Both exist in forms that challenge conventional definitions.

Neither fits comfortably into categories that humanity has traditionally accepted.

Neither spends much time worrying about that fact.

Their discussions frequently drift away from engineering or computer science toward philosophy.

What defines consciousness?

Can memory alone preserve identity?

Does changing one's body inevitably change one's self?

Neither claims to possess definitive answers.

Both appear unusually comfortable living without them.

Among all members of Base 32, Orpheus perhaps understands better than anyone what it means to continue existing after becoming something fundamentally different from what one once was.

This shared perspective creates a quiet bond between them.

One built not upon similarity of form, but upon similarity of experience.

# Unexplained Phenomena

On rare occasions, Zane experiences episodes that neither he nor anyone around him can adequately explain.

These moments are brief.

Sometimes they resemble distant memories.

Sometimes they appear to be fragments of conversations that never took place.

Occasionally he pauses as though hearing a familiar voice that no one else can perceive.

At other times he recognizes places or faces that should be entirely unknown to him.

The experiences are neither predictable nor progressively worsening.

They occur infrequently enough that Zane has learned to continue his work without drawing unnecessary attention to them.

Medical examinations reveal no evidence of neurological deterioration.

Cybernetic diagnostics report no hardware malfunction.

Psychological evaluation provides no definitive explanation.

Dr. Sophie Ouchet deliberately avoids assigning a diagnosis in the absence of sufficient evidence.

Professor Marchand considers the phenomenon scientifically intriguing but refuses to speculate beyond available observations.

VEGA concludes that the available data are internally inconsistent.

Orpheus questions whether the phenomenon is being interpreted through appropriate conceptual frameworks at all.

Zane himself rarely discusses the episodes.

When asked, he usually answers with characteristic simplicity.

*"Sometimes I remember things that never happened."*

He has never attempted to explain what he means.

Neither has anyone else.

# Internal Notes

Zane instinctively watches the sky whenever he enters an unfamiliar place.

He is comfortable with silence and never feels obliged to fill it with conversation.

He still keeps handwritten notebooks despite having perfect digital memory support.

He prefers repairing equipment himself whenever practical.

He often reads newly published scientific papers late at night with a cup of tea.

When thinking, he unconsciously checks the mechanical watch he has carried for decades, even though it no longer serves any practical purpose.

Although perfectly capable of using advanced technology, he remains surprisingly attached to physical books, printed photographs and handwritten notes.

He has never stopped considering himself a caretaker.

Even after returning from Fomalhaut.
